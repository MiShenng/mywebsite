  - "**Problem Addressed:** Traditional research in the humanities and social sciences relies on limited text samples, making it difficult to conduct systematic, quantifiable, and reproducible analyses of long-term cultural change. The authors attempt to use large-scale digitized book data to explore whether cultural phenomena can be measured and analyzed in the same way as objects of study in the natural sciences. This reminds me of Malevich’s new book, *Cultural Analytics*—though that one focuses more on images than text. I imagine it was inspired by this one!"
  - "**Methodology:** They constructed the Google Books corpus, which contains approximately 5.19 million digitized books, and tracked the evolution of language, ideas, figures, and social events within culture by analyzing the frequency of n-grams across different years. Their approach primarily involved descriptive time-series analysis and temporal comparisons."
  - "**Theoretical Framework:** This article introduces the concept of culturomics, which involves the quantitative study of cultural evolution using massive amounts of digital text. Its central hypothesis is that changes in word frequency within texts serve as observable traces of cultural and social memory, technological diffusion, and political processes, and are thus worthy of description and further analysis."
  - "**Core Conclusions:** 1. Vocabulary explosion 2. Evolution of English grammar 3. Accelerated decline of collective memory 4. Faster diffusion of technology 5. Faster rise to fame and faster fading from memory for celebrities 6. Censorship leaves traces in textual data… (These are all simple observations, but they offer insightful insights.)"
